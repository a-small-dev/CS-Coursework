# Scrum Master Reflection

## Overview
This journal reflects on the role and responsibilities of a Scrum Master and how I would apply Scrum events in a real project environment.

## Retrospective
When I think about leading a project as a Scrum Master, the first thing that comes to mind is making sure everyone knows the plan and feels supported. My approach would be pretty straightforward: keep the team in sync, address any concerns brought up (probably the main one being focused or stemming from the 5-week due date), and make sure each event (Sprint Planning, Daily Scrums, Backlog Refinement, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective) actually adds value instead of feeling like just another meeting.

For Sprint Planning, I’d start by reviewing the product backlog with the team and making sure everyone understands the priorities. I would encourage people to speak up about what they think is reasonable and doable for the sprint. The main benefit here is clarity, everyone leaves knowing what they’re responsible for. To make this event work well, I’d prepare ahead of time, maybe even have a rough estimate of tasks. According to Bass (2014), the Sprint Planner role is crucial for workload planning and helping the team triage user stories, so I’d lean on that idea to keep things focused.

Daily Scrums would be short, focused check-ins where each person shares what they did yesterday, what they’re doing today, and discuss any concerns that might arise. This brings out transparency, which is always beneficial. It keeps everyone on the same page and surfaces problems early. To make it successful, I would make sure it stays relatively short and encourage honesty, so people don’t sugarcoat issues. Kristensen and Paasivaara (2021) emphasize the “people person” side of the Scrum Master role, and I’d like to make sure team members feel comfortable sharing even small challenges.

With Backlog Refinement, I would work with the team to review upcoming tasks and make sure everything is clear. The benefit here is that it keeps the workflow smooth with less confusion and less wasted time during sprints. I would make this work by keeping it collaborative and not letting one person dominate the discussion. Bass (2014) notes that Scrum Masters can act as process anchors, which means helping the team stick to agile practices, and I’d use that approach to guide refinement without team members getting the feeling I’m trying to micromanage.

Sprint Review is about showing what we accomplished and getting feedback. The main benefit is checking if what we built actually meets expectations. I would make sure everyone on the team presents something, even small contributions, to keep morale up and show the team’s collective progress. I think this would also help give the feeling that the end goal is reachable and we are making steady progress towards it.

The Sprint Retrospective is a time to reflect and improve. Its benefit is promoting and finding ways for continuous improvement. I would probably keep it a bit more informal, maybe even use a few creative exercises to get people talking, and focus on actionable steps for the next sprint. 

Overall, following this Scrum framework helps the team stay coordinated, motivated, and efficient. The events give structure without being overly rigid or boring, and they create space for everyone to participate and feel valued. As Kristensen and Paasivaara (2021) point out, the invisible “people” side of the Scrum Master role (making sure the team is happy and the atmosphere is good) is just as important as the visible tasks like planning and coordination. In practice, that means checking in with team members individually if needed, encouraging collaboration, keeping the positive vibes going, and making sure no one feels overwhelmed.

A couple of steps I could take to ensure the team meets the goals of each event: I would try to stay organized, prepare materials in advance, and actively listen during meetings. I would also make sure to follow up on any action items that need immediate attention, provide feedback where needed, and generally try to keep morale high and the project on the right track.
